## Procurement Mechanisms

### 1. General Services Administration (GSA) Acquisition

**Role:** GSA is handling the acquisition of ICE detention facilities under the DRI program

**Evidence:**
- Multiple sources confirm GSA involvement in ICE facility purchases
- GSA spokesperson declined to clarify role, stating only: "GSA is following all lease procurement procedures in accordance with all applicable laws and regulations"
- Communities report frustration with GSA secrecy around acquisitions

**Procurement Authority:**
- Federal Property and Administrative Services Act of 1949
- 40 U.S.C. 553(b)(1) for correctional facilities
- FAR (Federal Acquisition Regulation) compliance required

### 2. SBA 8(a) Program (Small Business Administration)

**Discovery:** ICE is using the SBA 8(a) Business Development Program to bypass competitive bidding for contracts under $4.5 million

**Evidence from Other Facilities:**
- **El Paso, TX:** Joint venture between tribe-owned businesses using 8(a) program for mega-detention center
- **Oneida Nation:** Tribal entity awarded $6.4M in ICE contracts before nation barred further participation
- **Bering Straits Native Corporation:** Shareholders petitioning to divest from ICE contracts

**How It Works:**
- Federal agencies can award contracts to 8(a)-certified firms without competition if under $4.5M
- Tribal-owned businesses often qualify
- Allows rapid procurement but raises transparency concerns

**Recent Crackdowns:**
- **January 2026:** SBA suspended 1,000+ 8(a) firms following document request
- **February 2026:** SBA moved to terminate 150 D.C. 8(a) firms

**Implications for Orlando:**
- Construction/renovation contracts may be split into <$4.5M segments
- Operations contracts likely to exceed $4.5M threshold
- May use 8(a) for facility preparation, open bidding for operations

### 3. Emergency Detention Strategic Sourcing

**SAM.gov Solicitation Found:**

- **Purpose:** Pre-positioned contracts for rapid detention capacity expansion

**Purpose:** Allows ICE to quickly activate detention services without full procurement cycle

## Acquisition Mechanisms Available to ICE

### 1. Standard Competitive Procurement (FAR Part 15)

**Process:**
- RFP posted to SAM.gov
- 30-60 day response period
- Source selection and award
- Protest period

**Timeline:** 6-9 months  
**Use Case:** Operations contract ($20M+ annually)

### 2. GSA Schedule (FAR Part 8.4)

**Process:**
- Order against existing GSA Schedule contracts
- Limited competition (3 quotes for orders >$250K)

**Timeline:** 2-4 months  
**Use Case:** Construction, supplies, services on schedule

### 3. 8(a) Program (FAR Part 19.8)

**Process:**
- Direct award to 8(a)-certified firm
- No competition for awards <$4.5M
- SBA approval required

**Timeline:** 2-3 months  
**Use Case:** Renovation segments, specialty services

### 4. Urgent and Compelling (FAR Part 6.302)

**Process:**
- Justification & Approval (J&A) required
- Limited competition or sole source
- Congressional notification for >$75M

**Timeline:** 1-3 months  
**Use Case:** If "urgent and compelling" need documented

### 5. Other Transaction Authority (OTA)

**Process:**
- Non-FAR authority for prototype projects
- No competition required
- Flexibility in terms

**Timeline:** 1-2 months  
**Use Case:** Novel detention facility prototypes

## Risk Assessment

### High-Risk Procurement Indicators

**🚨 Red Flags Identified:**

1. **Extreme Secrecy:** GSA refuses to clarify role, property listings removed, owner silent
2. **Accelerated Timeline:** 9-month window from negotiation to activation is aggressive
3. **8(a) Program Abuse:** Pattern of using tribal entities to bypass competition
4. **Inadequate Planning:** Facility not zoned for habitation, raises safety concerns
5. **Political Pressure:** Congressional opposition may trigger oversight or protests

### Likely Procurement Challenges

**1. Competitive Protest Risk:**
- If sole-source award, likely protest from GEO Group, CoreCivic
- GAO protests add 100+ days to timeline
- Risk: Facility not operational by November 30 deadline

**2. Environmental Challenges:**
- Conversion from industrial to detention requires environmental review
- NEPA compliance may be required
- Risk: Delays if categorical exclusion not applicable

**3. Zoning and Permitting:**
- Industrial zoning may not permit residential detention
- City/County permitting required even if federal supremacy applies
- Risk: Local government resistance slows construction

**4. Safety Concerns:**
- Hurricane resistance requirements
- Waste management capacity
- Fire suppression for high-occupancy use
- Risk: Cost overruns or design changes

**5. Community Opposition:**
- Protests, legal challenges
- Negative media coverage
- Risk: Political pressure on contractors

### Mitigation Strategies (ICE Perspective)

**How ICE May Address Risks:**
1. Use **urgent and compelling** authority to justify sole-source
2. Claim **categorical exclusion** from NEPA for interior renovations
3. Assert **federal supremacy** to override local zoning
4. Engage **experienced contractor** (GEO Group) to manage conversion
5. Conduct **intensive community outreach** (unlikely given current approach)
