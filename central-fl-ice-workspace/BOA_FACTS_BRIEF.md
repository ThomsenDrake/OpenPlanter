# BOA Fact Sheet: Can Counties Be Forced?

**Quick Answer:** NO - Basic Ordering Agreements are 100% VOLUNTARY

---

## The Claim

**Orange County officials have said:** If the county terminates its IGSA (Intergovernmental Services Agreement) with the U.S. Marshals Service, it will be **forced** to sign a BOA (Basic Ordering Agreement) with ICE.

**This claim is LEGALLY FALSE.**

---

## The Reality

### BOAs Are Voluntary Under Federal Law

**Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR 16.703):**
> "A basic ordering agreement shall not state or imply any agreement by the Government to place future contracts or orders with the contractor..."

**National Sheriffs' Association:**
> "The Government is under no obligation to issue orders on a BOA, and **a service provider that enters into a BOA is not required to accept any order**."

### The Constitution Protects Counties

**Supreme Court (Printz v. United States, 1997):**
The federal government **cannot** compel state or local officials to administer federal programs. This is called the "anti-commandeering doctrine."

### Florida Law Doesn't Require BOAs

**Florida SB 168 requires:**
- ✓ Honoring ICE detainers (48-hour holds)
- ✓ Notifying ICE of release dates
- ✓ Providing inmate information
- ✓ Allowing ICE jail access

**Florida SB 168 does NOT require:**
- ✗ Signing BOAs
- ✗ Signing IGSAs
- ✗ Housing detainees beyond 48 hours
- ✗ Accepting federal detention contracts

---

## What Happens If Orange County Terminates IGSA?

### County CAN:
1. **Terminate IGSA** (March 31, 2026 deadline)
2. **Refuse BOA** (100% legal)
3. **Limit detention to 48-hour detainers only** (SB 168 minimum)
4. **Stop housing ICE detainees entirely** (beyond 48 hours)

### County CANNOT:
1. **Refuse to honor detainers** (SB 168 violation)
2. **Block ICE jail access** (SB 168 violation)
3. **Withhold inmate information** (SB 168 violation)

The county's BOA threat appears to be **negotiating leverage** with USMS, not legal reality.
