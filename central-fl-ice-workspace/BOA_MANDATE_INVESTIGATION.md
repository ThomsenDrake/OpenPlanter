# Investigation: Can Florida County Commissions Be Forced into BOAs?

**Date:** March 5, 2026  
**Status:** COMPLETE  
**Conclusion:** NO - BOAs are 100% VOLUNTARY  

---

## Executive Summary

**Finding:** County Commissions in Florida **CANNOT be forced** into Basic Ordering Agreements (BOAs) with the federal government if they terminate IGSA/287(g) agreements.

**Legal Basis:**
1. Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) explicitly states BOAs are voluntary
2. Supreme Court precedent (Printz v. United States) prohibits federal commandeering of local officials
3. No federal or Florida state law requires BOA participation
4. BOAs are procurement tools, not mandates

**Orange County's Claim:** County officials have presented BOA as "mandatory" if IGSA is terminated. **This claim is legally FALSE.**

---

## Detailed Legal Analysis

### 1. Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) - Primary Authority

**Source:** 48 CFR § 16.703 - Basic Ordering Agreements

**Key Provisions:**

> **(a) Description:** "A basic ordering agreement is a written instrument of understanding... A basic ordering agreement is **not a contract**."

> **(c) Limitations:** "A basic ordering agreement **shall not state or imply any agreement by the Government to place future contracts or orders** with the contractor..."

**National Sheriffs' Association FAQ on BOAs:**
> "The Government is under no obligation to issue orders on a BOA, and **a service provider that enters into a BOA is not required to accept any order**."

**Analysis:** BOAs are voluntary procurement mechanisms under federal contracting law. Neither party is obligated to enter into or fulfill orders under a BOA. The federal government cannot compel a county to sign a BOA any more than it can compel a private contractor to do so.

---

### 2. Constitutional Authority: Anti-Commandeering Doctrine

**Supreme Court Precedent:** *Printz v. United States*, 521 U.S. 898 (1997)

**Holding:** The federal government cannot compel state or local officials to administer federal regulatory programs.

**Key Quote (Justice Scalia, Majority Opinion):**
> "It is incontestible that the Constitution established a system of 'dual sovereignty.' Although the States surrendered many of their powers to the new Federal Government, they retained 'a residuary and inviolable sovereignty'... The power of the Federal Government would be augmented immeasurably if it were able to impress into its service—and at no cost to itself—the police officers of the 50 States."

**Application to BOAs:**
- A BOA is a federal procurement/detention program
- The federal government cannot force counties to participate
- Counties retain sovereignty over their jails and law enforcement resources
- The Tenth Amendment protects local autonomy in law enforcement decisions

**American Immigration Council Analysis:**
> "Federal actions intended to force local jurisdictions to perform immigration enforcement are likely unconstitutional. Under the Tenth Amendment of the U.S. Constitution, the federal government 'may neither issue directives requiring the States to address particular problems, nor command the States' officers, or those of their political subdivisions, to administer or enforce a federal regulatory program.'"

---

### 3. Florida State Law Analysis

**Florida SB 168 (2019) - "Anti-Sanctuary City" Law**

**What SB 168 Actually Requires:**
- Counties cannot have "sanctuary policies" that limit cooperation with federal immigration authorities
- Counties must honor ICE detainer requests (48-hour holds)
- Counties must provide inmate information to ICE
- Counties must allow ICE access to jails

**What SB 168 Does NOT Require:**
- ❌ Participation in specific federal programs (287(g), BOA, IGSA)
- ❌ Entering into contracts with federal agencies
- ❌ Housing ICE detainees beyond 48-hour detainer period
- ❌ Accepting federal reimbursement for detention

**Critical Distinction:** SB 168 requires "cooperation" with federal authorities, not "participation" in federal programs. A county can:
- Honor 48-hour detainers (required by state law)
- Notify ICE of release dates (required by state law)
- Provide inmate information (required by state law)
- BUT refuse to sign a BOA or IGSA for longer-term detention

**From PSL-Resources Analysis:**
> "Does Florida law require city police agencies or state law enforcement agencies to enter into 287(g) agreements? No. There is no state law requirement."

---

### 4. 287(g) Program - Separate from BOA

**Important Distinction:** 287(g) agreements and BOAs are DIFFERENT programs.

**287(g):**
- Deputizes local officers for immigration enforcement
- Has multiple models (Jail Enforcement, Warrant Service Officer, Task Force)
- Florida requires jails to participate (via SB 168 and 2022 law)
- Can be terminated with written notice to ICE

**BOA:**
- Procurement agreement for detention services
- Pays counties to hold ICE detainees
- 100% VOLUNTARY - no state or federal mandate
- Counties can refuse entirely

**Key Point:** A county can terminate a 287(g) agreement (if beyond minimum required) AND refuse a BOA. The only mandatory element under Florida law is honoring detainers, which doesn't require a BOA.

### 5. What Actually Happens If a County Terminates IGSA?

**Options Available to County Commissions:**

| Option | Legal Authority | Financial Impact |
|--------|----------------|------------------|
| **Terminate IGSA, Refuse BOA** | Permitted under FAR & Constitution | No federal reimbursement, but no detention costs either |
| **Terminate IGSA, Accept BOA** | Voluntary under FAR | $25/day vs $88/day (loss of revenue) |
| **Negotiate Better IGSA** | Permitted | Dependent on negotiation |
| **Continue IGSA as-is** | Permitted | $92/day deficit (Orange County situation) |

**What CANNOT Happen:**
- ❌ Federal government cannot force BOA signature
- ❌ State government cannot force BOA signature
- ❌ Courts cannot compel BOA participation

## Conclusion

**The claim that County Commissions can be forced into BOAs if they terminate IGSA/287(g) agreements is LEGALLY FALSE.**

**Basis:**
1. Federal procurement law (FAR) explicitly makes BOAs voluntary
2. Constitutional anti-commandeering doctrine (Printz) prohibits federal compulsion
3. No Florida state law requires BOA participation
4. BOAs are procurement tools, not regulatory mandates

**What Counties Must Do:**
- Honor 48-hour ICE detainers (Florida SB 168)
- Provide inmate information to ICE
- Allow ICE access to jails

**What Counties Can Refuse:**
- BOA agreements (100% voluntary)
- IGSA agreements (voluntary contracts)
- Long-term detention beyond 48-hour detainers
- Any federal reimbursement program

**Orange County's Position:**
The county's claim that BOA is "mandatory" appears to be political posturing for leverage in IGSA negotiations with USMS. Legally, the county has multiple options, including terminating IGSA and refusing BOA entirely, limiting detention to 48-hour detainers only.
