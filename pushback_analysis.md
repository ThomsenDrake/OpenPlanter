## Legal Framework: What Florida Law Actually Allows

### The Statute: § 119.07(1)(b), Florida Statutes

> "If the nature or volume of public records requested to be inspected, examined, or copied pursuant to this subsection is such as to require **extensive use of information technology resources** or **extensive clerical or supervisory assistance** by personnel of the agency involved, or both, the agency may charge, in addition to the actual cost of duplication, a **special service charge, which shall be reasonable** and shall be based on **the cost incurred for such extensive use of information technology resources or the labor cost of the personnel providing the service that is actually incurred by the agency** or attributable to the agency for the clerical and supervisory assistance required, or both."

### Key Legal Principles (from AGO 2000-11 and statutory text):

1. **The charge must be "reasonable."** This is a statutory requirement, not a suggestion.
2. **The charge must reflect "the labor cost of the personnel providing the service that is actually incurred."** This means actual cost, not inflated estimates.
3. **The charge is limited to "extensive clerical or supervisory assistance."** The statute specifically uses the words "clerical" and "supervisory" — not "administrative," "executive," or "managerial."
4. **AGO 2000-11** states the policy should "reflect no more than the actual cost of the personnel's time and be sensitive to accommodating the request in such a way as to ensure unfettered access."
5. **The burden is on the agency** to demonstrate that the nature/volume of records actually requires this level of effort.

## Arguments for Pushing Back

### 1. DEMAND A DETAILED TASK-BY-TASK BREAKDOWN

The invoice provides only generic labels ("Excessive Administrative Time") with no explanation of what each person would actually be doing. You have a right to understand what you're paying for.

**Ask specifically:**
- What tasks will the MicroTransit Division Manager perform for **18 hours**?
- What tasks require a **Director of Budget** (3 hrs @ $122.50/hr)?
- What tasks require a **Director of Communications** (5 hrs @ $124.52/hr)?
- Is any of this time for **legal review or exemption determination**? If so, that is NOT a chargeable cost — agencies cannot pass the cost of their legal obligation to determine exemptions onto the requester.

### 2. CHALLENGE THE USE OF HIGH-SALARIED PERSONNEL

This is your strongest argument. The statute authorizes charges for **"clerical or supervisory assistance"** — not executive-level administrative review.

**Key points:**
- The MicroTransit Division Manager ($78.40/hr) and two Directors ($122.50 and $124.52/hr) account for **$2,401.30 of the $3,168.89 labor charges (75.5%)**.
- Tasks like searching email databases, pulling files, compiling documents, and copying records are **clerical functions** that do not require Director-level or Division Manager-level personnel.
- If the County chooses to assign expensive administrators to do work that clerical staff could perform, **the requester should not bear the inflated cost of that staffing decision**.
- The appropriate charge should be based on the rate of the **lowest-paid employee capable of performing the task**, consistent with the statutory requirement that charges be "reasonable."
- Even if a manager must review records for exempt/confidential content, the **time spent determining what is exempt is part of the agency's legal obligation** and courts have questioned whether that cost can be passed to the requester.

### 3. CHALLENGE THE 18 HOURS FOR THE MICROTRANSIT DIVISION MANAGER

This single line item ($1,411.20) is the largest charge — nearly half the total labor cost. 

**Ask:**
- What specifically requires 18 hours of the Division Manager's time (more than two full workdays)?
- Is the Manager reviewing emails for responsiveness? That's an email search function (IT) or clerical function.
- Is the Manager reviewing for exempt content? That's a legal determination the County must make regardless — it shouldn't be charged to the requester.
- Is the Manager the only person with knowledge of the program? Even so, their time should be limited to identifying where records are located, not performing the search and retrieval itself.

### 4. QUESTION WHETHER THIS IS AN ESTIMATE OR ACTUAL COST

The statute requires the charge to be based on cost **"actually incurred."** An inflated upfront estimate may not reflect actual costs.

**Ask:**
- Is this an estimate or a guaranteed cost?
- Will you receive a refund if the actual time is less than estimated?
- Will the County provide a detailed time log showing actual hours worked upon completion?

### 5. NARROW OR PHASE YOUR REQUEST TO REDUCE COST

Many of the records in your original request should be **readily available with minimal effort**:

**Phase 1 — Low/No Cost Records (request these separately or first):**
- The RFP 604918-25/PCD itself (standard procurement document — should be on file)
- All vendor proposals received (maintained by Purchasing)
- The executed contract with BeFree LLC and amendments
- BCC meeting agendas, minutes, and presentations (likely already digitized/online)
- Budget documents and financial records for the Scout program

These are discrete, already-compiled documents that should require minimal search time. **The County should not charge special service fees for records that are readily accessible.**

**Phase 2 — The Expensive Email Searches:**
The bulk of the cost is almost certainly driven by your five email search requests. Consider:
- **Narrowing search terms**: Reduce the number of OR combinations. Each additional term multiplies the results and review time.
- **Narrowing the date range**: Your broadest searches span June 2024 to present (~21 months). Tightening these reduces volume.
- **Narrowing the accounts searched**: Instead of searching entire divisions, identify specific individuals.
- **Accepting electronic native format**: You already requested this — confirm it reduces production time vs. PDF conversion.
- **Dropping the lowest-priority search**: Search 5 (public inquiries) could be deferred.

### 6. REQUEST ELECTRONIC DELIVERY TO ELIMINATE THE USB DRIVE COST

If records can be delivered via email, secure file transfer, or cloud link, the $10 USB drive charge is unnecessary. Florida law (§ 119.07(1)(a)) states the custodian "shall furnish a copy of the record upon payment of the fee prescribed by law" — and electronic delivery is generally the cheapest method.

### 7. EXERCISE YOUR RIGHT TO INSPECT RATHER THAN COPY

Under § 119.07(1)(a), you have the right to **inspect** records at no charge (except special service charges for extensive assistance). You could:
- Request to inspect the email search results on-site before paying for copies
- This lets you identify which records you actually want copied, potentially reducing the volume and cost dramatically

### 8. FILE A COMPLAINT WITH THE AG'S MEDIATION PROGRAM

If the County refuses to negotiate, the **Florida Attorney General's Open Government Mediation Program** (§ 16.60, Fla. Stat.) provides free, voluntary mediation:
- Contact: Office of the Attorney General, (850) 245-0140
- Website: myfloridalegal.com → Open Government
- This is a non-adversarial process that can be very effective

### 9. CHECK WHAT'S ALREADY PUBLICLY AVAILABLE

Before paying anything, check:
- **Seminole County BCC meeting portal**: Agendas, minutes, and recordings are typically posted online — you may not need to request these through PRR at all.
- **County procurement portal**: RFP documents and awarded contracts are often posted publicly.
- **Seminole County budget documents**: Annual budgets are public and usually posted online.
- Every item you can obtain through public portals is one less item on the invoice.
